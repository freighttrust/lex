                Transport of waste: transfrontier

                by Practical Law Environment
                Related Content

                This note summarises the regime for transfrontier shipment (international transport) of waste.

                Scope of this note

                This note summarises the controls on the transfrontier (international) shipment of waste that have been agreed at both an international and EU level and implemented by the UK. In particular:
                • Waste is classified according to how hazardous it is.
                • Transfrontier shipment of waste must comply with requirements for notification, movement documents, packaging, labelling and transport.
                • Shipments of waste to certain countries are prohibited.
                • Export countries must ensure that waste can be dealt with in an environmentally sound manner.
                • Countries receiving waste may be able to object to planned shipments.
                This note does not cover the transfrontier shipment of radioactive waste in any detail (see Transfrontier shipment of radioactive waste below).
                For more information on the:
                • Domestic transport of waste, see Practice note, Transport of waste: regulation of carriers, brokers and dealers.
                • Hazardous waste regime, see Practice note, Hazardous waste regime.
                • Practical Law's other materials on waste, see Waste regime toolkit.

                Impact of Brexit

                Overview

                There will be a legislative review process following the UK’s decision to leave the EU (Brexit). Trade in waste with the EU (and so its transfrontier shipment) is important because the UK's reprocessing industry cannot process every kind of waste material produced by households and businesses. The UK also takes in certain other types of waste, notably from Ireland. To allow the trade in waste to continue easily (including allowing UK businesses to continue to provide their expertise in the EU) some form of alignment with the EU or international rules that allow trading in waste will need to be negotiated or confirmed by the UK. However, there is also an opportunity to change the substance and enforcement of other rules that govern the waste and environmental services sector.
                The UK will need to continue to comply with the international Basel Convention on the control of transboundary movements of hazardous waste and their disposal (see Basel Convention below) and the Decision C(2001)107/Final concerning the control of transboundary movements of wastes destined for recovery operations as amended by Decision C(2004)20 (OECD Decision) (see OECD Decision on control of transboundary movements of waste below) once the Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 14 June 2006 on shipments of waste (EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006), which incorporates both these sets of rules, is no longer directly applicable (see EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 below). Also, the UK may need to continue to comply with the waste management framework in the Waste Framework Directive 2008 (Directive 2008/98/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 19 November 2008 on waste and repealing certain Directives) as divergence between the definitions of waste may hamper the trade in waste and increase waste export prices. This is particularly important in light of changes in China concerning trade in waste. Although there may be benefits to continuing to comply with other EU waste legislation (for example, concerning batteries, used cars, sewage and removal of offshore oil platforms) it has been argued that alignment is not vital.
                For more information on the impact of Brexit on environmental law generally, see Practice note, Brexit: the effect on environmental law.

                No-deal scenario technical notice and guidance

                UK government no-deal notice and guidance

                In February 2019, the Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs (Defra) published Importing and exporting waste if there’s no Brexit deal, which largely confirms the information in the government's (withdrawn) October 2018 technical notice (see Legal update, No-deal Brexit: maintaining transboundary waste shipments: technical notice). It sets out the practical procedure for waste shipments from the UK to the EU, and vice versa, including duly reasoned requests and customs declarations.
                The guidance also confirms that the UK government has secured agreement that most of UK consents for shipments of notifiable waste that went beyond exit day would be rolled over. This follows the process set out by the European Commission in its notice to stakeholders as explained below.
                (See Legal update, No-deal Brexit: government publishes guidance on importing and exporting waste.)
                In February 2019, the UK government also published separate guidance on shipping radioactive waste and spent fuel after a no-deal Brexit. The guidance states that if the UK leaves the EU without a deal, operators may need to obtain new authorisations to ship radioactive waste or spent fuel. Operators should contact the relevant competent authority (the Environment Agency (EA) in England, the Scottish Environmental Protection Agency (SEPA), Natural Resources Wales (NRW) or the Northern Ireland Environment Agency) to check whether their existing authorisations are valid.
                There will be new application forms to allow operators without an existing authorisation to apply to the relevant competent authority and new documents to accompany each shipment, which will be published in March 2019. It will still be a criminal offence to ship radioactive waste and spent fuel without valid authorisation and documentation.
                Operators must notify the relevant competent authority of the arrival of shipments using a new notification form that will also be published in March 2019.
                (See Legal update, UK government publishes guidance on shipping radioactive waste and spent fuel after a No-deal Brexit.)

                European Commission notice to stakeholders

                In November 2018, the European Commission updated its notice to stakeholders, Withdrawal of the UK and EU waste law stating that if the UK leaves the EU without a deal, import/export licences issued by the UK would no longer be valid for shipments of waste to the remaining EU countries, and licenses issued by the EU would no longer be valid for shipments to the UK, from the day the UK leaves.
                Therefore, current approvals to ship notified waste between the UK and the EU or to transit the EU that extend beyond exit day would be subject to a re-approval process to ensure continuity. There is currently no process set out in the EU Waste Shipment Regulation 2006 to deal with this scenario of how notified shipments that have already been approved by UK and EU competent authorities should be re-approved.
                However, as explained in Defra's guidance, the UK government has agreed a rollover of existing notified shipments with most member states.

                Waste shipments from the UK to the EU

                The UK will remain a party to the Basel Convention and a member of the OECD following Brexit. It will continue to implement those requirements.
                In a no-deal scenario, the UK would be treated in the same way as any other OECD country, or any country that is party to the Basel Convention, looking to export waste to an EU country. UK exporters would need to familiarise themselves with customs guidelines that the EU has laid down for imports of waste from outside the EU.
                Under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, OECD countries wishing to export waste to the EU for disposal must submit a duly reasoned request (DRR) to the relevant EU competent authority, explaining why the country does not have and cannot reasonably acquire the appropriate disposal facilities. The UK government would need to submit DRRs for any exports to the EU of waste for disposal, before a notification to export could be submitted by a UK exporter to the relevant UK competent authority. In most cases, the export of UK waste for disposal is already prohibited so the impact of this additional step is unlikely to be significant.
                There would be no changes to the procedure for exports of waste for recycling that are eligible to be shipped under the Green Control procedure as laid down in the OECD Decision and the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006.

                Waste shipments from the EU to the UK

                In a no-deal scenario, the UK would be treated in the same way as any other OECD country looking to import waste from an EU country and would continue to apply the procedures laid down in the Basel Convention and the OECD Decision.
                Under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, EU states would be prohibited from exporting waste for disposal, or exporting mixed municipal waste for recovery, to the UK.
                There would be no changes to the procedure for imports of waste for recycling that are eligible to be shipped under the Green Control procedure as laid down in the OECD Decision and the EU Waste Shipment Regulation 2006.

                Ireland

                The Defra technical notice indicates that the UK government is clear that in a no-deal scenario, it must respect the UK's unique relationship with Ireland, in sharing a land border and as co-signatories of the Belfast Agreement. The UK government has consistently placed upholding the Belfast Agreement at the heart of its approach and recognises it has provided for the deep economic and social cooperation on the island of Ireland. This includes North-South cooperation between Northern Ireland and Ireland, which the UK government is committed to protecting in line with the letter and spirit of Strand two of the Agreement.
                The Irish government has indicated that it would need to discuss arrangements in the event of no deal with the European Commission and EU member states. The UK would stand ready to engage constructively to meet our commitments and act in the best interests of the people of Northern Ireland, recognising the very significant challenges that the lack of a UK-EU legal agreement would pose in this unique and highly sensitive context.

                Brexit legislation

                Basel Convention and OECD Decision regimes legislation

                The International Waste Shipments (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 were made on 14 March 2019 and will come into force on exit day. The Regulations are made under section 8 of the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 (EUWA), which enables a minister to make regulations to deal with deficiencies in retained EU law arising from withdrawal (see Practice note, European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018: legislating for Brexit). They amend retained EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, Transfrontier Shipment of Waste Regulations 2007 (SI 2007/1711) and related EU legislation on international waste shipments on exit day.
                The Regulations:
                • Make minor and technical amendments to ensure that the current regulatory regime on the control of international waste shipments can continue to function on exit day.
                • Ensure that the UK will continue to implement its obligations under the Basel Convention, and the OECD Decision on control of transfrontier movements of wastes.
                • Remove the requirement for the government to report annually to the European Commission.
                For more information, see Legal updates, International Waste Shipments (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 made: Brexit SI and Government publishes draft International Waste Shipments (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2018: Brexit SI.
                Additionally, on 8 January 2019, the draft Environment and Wildlife (Legislative Functions) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 were laid before Parliament and are due to come into force on exit day. The draft Regulations are also made under section 8 of the EUWA.
                The draft Regulations amend retained EU legislation relating to transfrontier shipments of waste (among others) on exit day. The draft Regulations remove references to the European Commission and the delegated and implementing acts that it can make (EU tertiary legislation). Where appropriate, they transfer the legislative functions currently conferred upon the European Commission to the appropriate UK authority and convert EU procedures to UK procedures. This ensures that the relevant regimes can continue to function smoothly after Brexit, without the need for primary legislation when a change in technical matters is required.
                (See Legal update, Draft Environment and Wildlife (Legislative Functions) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 laid before Parliament: Brexit SI.)

                Transfrontier shipments of radioactive substances

                The Shipments of Radioactive Substances (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/571) were made on 13 March 2019 and will come into force on exit day. The Regulations are made under section 8 of the EUWA.
                The Regulations revoke retained Regulation (Euratom) No 1493/93 on shipments of radioactive substances between member states on exit day, and put in place new domestic regulations so that the existing regime in relation to the import of radioactive substances into the UK from EU member states can continue to function after the UK leaves the Euratom Treaty on exit day.
                The Regulations:
                • Ensure the requirement on consignees of sealed radioactive sources to make prior written declarations demonstrating that they have complied with national requirements for the safe storage, use and disposal of the substance before any shipment from the EU to the UK can take place, will continue to apply after Brexit. Declaration must be sent by the consignee to the relevant environment agency in the UK.
                • Place the obligation to make prior written declarations on the consignee in the UK.
                • No longer requires source holders of radioactive substances to submit a quarterly return detailing all of the shipments made during the preceding quarter to the relevant competent authority in the destination member state.
                The Transfrontier Shipment of Radioactive Waste and Spent Fuel (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/156) were made on 31 January 2019 and will come into force on exit day. The Regulations are made under section 8 of the EUWA.
                The Regulations revoke the Transfrontier Shipment of Radioactive Waste and Spent Fuel Regulations 2008 (SI 2008/3087), which implement the Council Directive 2006/117/Euratom on the supervision and control of shipments of radioactive waste and spent fuel. and Council Directive 96/29/Euratom laying down basic safety standards for the protection of the health of workers and the general public against the dangers arising from ionizing radiation. They put in place provisions replicating the procedures for the supervision and control of shipments between the UK and other countries. The Regulations also revoke Commission Decision 2008/312/Euratom, which established standard EU-wide documentation for shipments referred to in the 2006 Directive. The government will publish replacement documentation and guidance.
                The Regulations:
                • Correct deficiencies and update references in the legislation implementing the Directives, which will arise after the UK leaves the Euratom Treaty on exit day. They ensure the continuation of regulation and authorisation of shipment of radioactive waste and spent fuel after Brexit.
                • Ensure the UK can continue to implement its obligations as a party to the international Joint Convention on the Safety of Spent Fuel Management and on the Safety of Radioactive Waste management.
                • Provide that UK importers will have to contact authorities in Euratom states to request authorisation, evidence their agreement with the exporter and notify the authority of completion of the shipment.
                For more information on both sets of regulations, see:
                • Legal updates, Shipments of Radioactive Substances (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 made: Brexit SI and Transfrontier Shipment of Radioactive Waste and Spent Fuel (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 made: Brexit SI.
                • Practice note, Brexit and energy: Impact of Brexit on nuclear energy and Euratom.

                Ship recycling (hazardous materials)

                The Ship Recycling (Facilities and Requirements for Hazardous Materials on Ships) (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 (SI 2019/277) were made on 12 February 2019 and come into force on exit day. The regulations are made under section 8 of the EUWA. They amend existing statutory instruments and retained EU legislation relating to ship recycling and the authorisation of ship recycling facilities on exit day. In particular, they amend the EU Ship Recycling Regulation 2013 (Regulation (EU) 1257/2013 of the European Parliament and of the Council on ship of 20 November 2013 on ship recycling and amending Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 and Directive 2009/16/EC), which implements the rules of the 2009 Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships. The main provisions of the EU regulation will apply on 31 December 2018 (see EU Ship Recycling Regulation 2013 below).
                The regulations make necessary amendments to ensure that the existing legal framework on ship recycling can continue to operate on exit day. They replace references to EU member states with references to the UK and amend other wording to reflect that the UK will no longer be an EU member state.
                They also:
                • Establish an equivalent UK list of approved facilities to replace the EU's approved European list which UK flagged ships must use. The Secretary of State will have powers to accept or remove facilities on the UK list. There will also be a new procedure allowing ship recycling facilities worldwide to apply for the inclusion onto the UK approved list.
                • Remove redundant requirements on the UK to report to the European Commission.
                • Transfer powers from the European Commission to the Secretary of State to make tertiary legislation. The Secretary of State will also be able to update legislation in line with international requirements.
                (See Legal updates, Government publishes draft Ship Recycling (Facilities and Requirements for Hazardous Materials on Ships) (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019: Brexit SI and Ship Recycling (Facilities and Requirements for Hazardous Materials on Ships) (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 made: Brexit SI.)

                International legislation

                The key international legislation regulating the transfrontier shipment of waste is the:
                • Basel Convention on the control of transboundary movements of hazardous waste and their disposal (see Basel Convention below).
                • OECD decision on control of transboundary movements of waste (see OECD decision on control of transboundary movements of waste below).
                The Basel Convention and OECD Decision are implemented in the EU by an EU Regulation (see EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 below). The EU Regulation is directly applicable in the UK.
                This note briefly explains the international legislation, in order to put the EU and UK legislation in context.

                Basel Convention

                Objectives

                The Basel Convention on the control of transboundary movements of hazardous waste and their disposal (Basel Convention) came into force in May 1992 as a response to unregulated toxic trading in hazardous waste from more regulated countries to less regulated developing and Eastern European countries.
                The main objectives of the Basel Convention are to:
                • Minimize the generation of hazardous wastes (both in terms of quantity produced and how hazardous the waste is).
                • Dispose of hazardous waste as close to the source of production as possible.
                • Reduce the movement of hazardous wastes.

                Main requirements

                The Basel Convention includes the following requirements:
                • The state of export must notify the competent authorities of the states of import and transit of hazardous and other wastes, and obtain their prior written consent.
                • Each shipment must be accompanied by a movement document, from the point at which a transboundary movement begins to the point of disposal. Hazardous waste shipments made without a movement document are illegal.
                • Signatory states must control the waste streams set out in Annexes I to III to the Convention.
                • The exporting state must ensure that all wastes (for disposal or recovery) can be dealt with in an environmentally sound manner. If the exporting state believes that waste will not be dealt with in this way, it has a duty to arrange for the return of such waste.
                • Hazardous wastes that are to be exported must be packaged, labelled and transported in accordance with recognised international standards.
                The Basel Convention allows signatory states to:
                • Ban the import of hazardous waste if they inform other parties of that decision.
                • Agree bilateral arrangements for trade in hazardous waste. However, arrangements with non-parties are prohibited, unless they can ensure the environmentally sound management of the waste. An exception is that transboundary movements can take place, if the state of export is not capable of managing or disposing of the hazardous waste in an environmentally sound manner.

                Amendment to ban exports of hazardous waste

                In 1995, an amendment to the Basel Convention was agreed (ban amendment), which required all exports of hazardous wastes be prohibited from countries listed in a proposed new annex (Annex VII) to the Convention (which would cover EU member states, OECD countries and Liechtenstein) to all other parties to the Basel Convention. The ban would apply initially to waste shipped for disposal and then, from 1998, for recovery or recycling (see Legal update, Ban on hazardous waste exports).
                The ban amendment has not yet entered into force, despite having been ratified by 69 parties (including the EU and the UK). This is because there is a dispute as to whether the amendment has to be ratified by three-quarters of the parties who accepted the amendment or three-quarters of the parties to the Basel Convention.
                Lists of hazardous and non-hazardous waste covered by the ban amendment and to be annexed to the Basel Convention were agreed in 1998.
                For more information, see Basel Convention: Status of Ratifications - Ban Amendment.

                Extension to plastic waste

                In May 2019, the Conferences of Parties to the Basel, Rotterdam and Stockholm Conventions (Triple COPs) agreed to extend the Basel Convention to plastic waste so that states exporting plastic waste must obtain prior written consent from the competent authorities of the states of import and transit (see Legal update, Decisions on exports of plastic waste and on chemicals in Conferences of Parties to the Basel, Rotterdam and Stockholm Conventions).

                Liability and compensation system

                In 1999, a protocol to the Basel Convention was signed, which establishes a liability and compensation system for damage from accidents resulting from the transfrontier shipment of waste (Basel Protocol on Liability and Compensation for Damage Resulting from Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal).
                The protocol will enter into force after 20 parties have ratified it. The UK has signed the protocol but needs to introduce legislation in order to be able to ratify it.

                Implementation in the EU and UK

                The EU has implemented the main provisions of the Basel Convention and the ban amendment through an EU Regulation that has direct effect in member states (see EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 below).

                More information

                For more information on the Basel Convention, the Ban Amendment and the Protocol, see Basel Convention: Overview.

                OECD Decision on control of transboundary movements of waste

                The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) Decision C(2001)107/Final concerning the control of transboundary movements of wastes destined for recovery operations as amended by Decision C(2004)20 (OECD Decision) sets out a control system for all wastes (not just hazardous wastes) that are transported between OECD member countries for recovery. It revised an earlier OECD Decision (Decision C(92)39/Final concerning the control of transfrontier movements of wastes destined for recovery operations).
                The OECD Decision classifies waste and requires notification for shipment of different types of waste, as follows:
                • Green list (non-hazardous) waste.
                • Amber list waste (requiring prior notification to, but not consent from, the receiving country). This list has subsequently been merged with the red list.
                • Red list waste (requiring prior notification and consent). This list has subsequently been merged with the amber list.
                The level of regulation under the OECD's control system depends on the classification of the waste.
                This regime has been incorporated into EU legislation (see EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 below).
                For more information, see OECD: Control system for waste recovery.

                EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006

                Summary of Regulation

                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 came into force in July 2007 (Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 14 June 2006 on shipments of waste).
                It repealed Council Regulation (EEC) No 259/93 of 1 February 1993 on the supervision and control of shipments of waste within, into and out of the European Community.
                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 applies to all international waste shipments for all purposes. Therefore, it applies to waste shipments covered by the:
                • Basel Convention (including implementing the ban amendment into EU law, that is, banning the export of all hazardous waste from EU member states to non-OECD countries) (see Basel Convention above).
                • OECD control system, including classification into green, amber and red list wastes. In addition, it increases the provisions that apply to green list waste shipments for recovery (see OECD decision on control of transboundary movements of waste above).
                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 is complicated as it regulates the movement of waste based on:
                • The type of waste, that is, whether it is hazardous or not.
                • Whether the waste is being shipped for disposal or recovery purposes.
                • The destination country of the waste. Different provisions apply according to the destination (for example, whether the destination is another EU member state, an OECD country or a non-OECD country, or a signatory to the Basel Convention).
                To determine what controls apply to a proposed shipment of waste under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, it is necessary to consider all three factors set out above.
                The controls applied by the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 include:
                • Prohibition of certain shipments (see Prohibition below).
                • A requirement for prior written notification and consent before certain shipments take place (see Classification of waste and prior written notification below).
                • A requirement for certain information to accompany the waste shipment, sometimes known as Article 18 controls (see Tracking and Article 18 controls below).

                Amendment to change classification, documentation and on waste streams

                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 has been amended a number of times. Many of the amendments relate to:
                • Changes in the classification of wastes.
                • Notification and movement documentation.
                • Specific waste streams.
                For more information on some of the key recent amendments, see:
                • EU Ship Recycling Regulation 2013 below.
                • Legal update, Shipment of waste: European Commission Regulation including certain unclassified wastes in Annex IIIB published in the Official Journal.
                • Legal update, Shipment of waste: European Commission Regulation published in Official Journal.
                The Commission has published:
                • A list of amending legislation, see Commission: Environment: Community legislation on shipment of waste.
                • Background information, see Commission: Waste shipments.

                Amendment to strengthen inspection of waste shipments

                On 27 June 2014, Regulation (EU) 660/2014 of the European Parliament (EP) and of the Council of 15 May 2014 amending Regulation 1013/2006 of the EP and of the Council of 14 June 2006 on shipments of waste, was published in the Official Journal, which strengthens the provisions on inspection of waste shipments in the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 to ensure more uniform implementation of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 throughout the EU.
                In particular, it requires member states to adopt inspection plans by 1 January 2017. Inspection plans should set out the objectives of inspections, the area that the inspection plan covers and the tasks assigned to each authority involved in inspections.
                The amendments may have been fuelled by the high-profile case where Trafigura was found guilty under Dutch law of transferring toxic waste to the Ivory Coast for disposal (see Legal update, Dutch court finds Trafigura guilty of transferring toxic waste to Ivory Coast). In a related case, the Dutch Court of Appeal decided that the Dutch public prosecutor was not obliged to prosecute Trafigura for its activities in disposing of toxic waste in Ivory Coast (see Legal update, Dutch court decides not to prosecute Trafigura for disposal of hazardous waste in Ivory Coast).
                For more information, see Legal updates, Shipment of waste: Regulation strengthening inspections on waste shipments published in Official Journal and Shipment of waste: European Commission proposal to strengthen inspections on waste shipments through amendment of Waste Shipment Regulation.

                Classification of waste and prior written notification

                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 classifies waste into green and amber categories:
                • Green list waste is essentially non-hazardous waste, and is generally subject to a lower level of control (Annex III).
                • Amber list waste (notifiable waste) requires prior notification and consent before it is shipped (Annex IV).
                The Environment Agency (EA) (for England) has produced an unofficial consolidated version of the relevant Annexes (see EA: Unofficial consolidated version of Annexes III, IIIA, IIIB IV & IVA of Regulation (EC) No. 1013/2006 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 14 June 2006 on shipments of wastes).

                Procedure for prior written notification

                Procedure

                Articles 4 to 17 of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 set out how notification should be carried out.
                The key steps are as follows:
                • The notifier has to submit their prior written notification to and through the competent authority in the country of dispatch. The concept of notifier is defined in Article 3(15), and will be a different person depending on the circumstances. Broadly, the notifier is the person or organisation who intends to carry out the shipment (usually, a producer, collector, dealer, broker or holder of the waste).
                • The competent authority of dispatch must send on a valid notification to the competent authority in the countries of destination and transit within three working days of receipt.
                • Additional information can be required by the competent authorities within three working days of receiving the notification (Article 8).
                • Once the competent authority in the country of destination is satisfied the notification has been properly completed it must (within three working days) send the notifier an acknowledgement of receipt and send copies to the other competent authorities.
                • The various competent authorities have 30 days from the date of the acknowledgement to decide whether to object or consent to the shipment (with or without conditions). For information on the grounds for objections, see Objections to shipments below.
                • Consents to shipment expire after a year of being issued.
                • Final disposal or recovery has to be completed no later than one year after the receipt of the waste. The facility carrying out those operations has to certify that they have taken place.
                The notification document (set out in Annex IA) must be accompanied by:
                • A movement document (set out in Annex IB).
                • A concluded contract with the consignee of the waste (or evidence of such a contract).
                • Evidence of, or a declaration that, the notifier has in place a financial guarantee or equivalent insurance that will cover the costs of transport and storage if the waste has to be returned to the country of dispatch.
                A general notification can be made where multiple shipments of the same waste are being made to the same consignee, facility and by the same route (Article 13).
                Some exemptions to the usual prior notice procedure apply under Article 1, such as waste generated on a ship until it is offloaded for recovery or disposal.

                Cases

                In SC Total Waste Recycling SRL v Országos Környezetvédelmi és Természetvédelmi Főfelügyelőség [[2015] EUECJ C-487/14, the Court of Justice (ECJ) decided that a waste carrier had to provide information about a change to its border crossing point for a shipment of "amber list" waste to the competent authorities under the notification requirements (see Legal update, Transfrontier shipment of waste regime requires notification of change to border entry point (ECJ)).
                In Nutrivet D.O.O.E.L. v Országos Környezetvédelmi és Természetvédelmi Fofelügyeloség [2016] EUECJ C-69/15, the ECJ decided that a shipment of waste would be deemed an illegal shipment of waste under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 where the waste carrier had incorrectly completed the required accompanying documents, even though the correct information was contained in other documents that were available to the accompanying authority (see Legal update, A transfrontier shipment of waste will be classified as illegal if accompanying documents are incorrect (ECJ)).
                In Conti 11. Container Schiffahrts-GmbH & Co. KG MS "MSC Flaminia" v Land Niedersachsen (Case C-689/17), the ECJ clarified the status of waste that results from an accident on board a ship, in the context of the prior notification requirement. Article 3 of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 requires prior written notification and consent before shipments of certain types of waste to another country. However, waste that is generated on board a ship is excluded from the Regulation's notification and consent requirements until it is offloaded for recovery or disposal (Article 1(3)(b)). A fire broke out on a container ship travelling from the US to Belgium. The ship was towed into German waters. The German authorities required the ship owner to carry out a notification procedure in respect of the scrap metal and fire-extinguishing water mixed with sludge and cargo residues on the ship. The ECJ said that, taking into account the context and purpose of the Regulation, the exclusion under Article 1(3)(b) applies to waste generated on board a ship, irrespective of the circumstance in which such waste is generated. Therefore, the exception applied to the waste that resulted from the fire in this case (see Legal update, Waste resulting from fire on board a ship is excluded from usual EU Waste Shipments Regulation notification requirements (ECJ)).

                Objections to shipments

                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 allow a country of dispatch or a country of destination to object to a proposed shipment that is destined for disposal (Article 11) or recovery (Article 12).
                There are a number of grounds of objection available. These are broadly similar, whether the shipment is destined for disposal or recovery.
                Countries of transit are also able to raise objections under both Articles 11 and 12, but only a few of the grounds for objection may be used.
                The objection must be raised within 30 days following an acknowledgement under Article 8 by the competent authority in the country of destination of the notification. If the problems giving rise to the objection are not resolved within the 30-day window for objection, the notification ceases to be valid. If the notifier still intends to make the shipment, a new notification has to be submitted (unless all the competent authorities involved agree otherwise).
                An example of one of the grounds for objection is that the planned shipment, disposal or recovery would breach national legislation on environmental protection, public order, public safety or health protection concerning actions taken in the objecting country.
                A controversial issue raised in the courts is the extent to which a member state can impose its own environmental standards on shipments destined for other member states. This issue has been considered in:
                • Chemische Afvalstoffen Dusseldorp BV and others v Minister van Volkshuisvesting, Ruimtelijke Ordening en Milieubeheer (Case C-203/96) [1998] EUECJ C-203/96.
                • DaimlerChrysler AG v Land Baden-Wurttemberg (Case C-324/99) [2001] EUECJ C-324/99.
                • EU-Wood-Trading GmbH v Sonderabfall-Management-Gesellschaft Rheinland-Pfalz mbH (Case C-277/02) [2004] EUECJ C-277/02 (EU-Wood case).
                In the first two cases, the then Court of Justice of the European Union (ECJ) did not allow objections from the states of dispatch that were based on their own environmental laws being more stringent. However, in the EU-Wood case, the ECJ allowed an objection that the concentrations of lead in contaminated wood to be exported for recovery from Germany to Italy exceeded German national guidelines, as recovery in Italy might breach the obligations in the Waste Framework Directive.
                For more information, see Commission: Case law of the European Court of Justice with particular relevance to waste shipments.
                Article 12(1)(c) allows objections to a shipment for recovery where the proposed recovery would not meet the recovery standards in the state of dispatch (although this is subject to some qualifications). However, there is no equivalent provision for shipments for disposal.

                Prohibition

                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 prohibits the shipment of waste in several circumstances, including:
                • Exports of waste for disposal, from an EU member state to a non-member state. However, this does not apply if the destination is a European Free Trade Association (EFTA) country that is a party to the Basel Convention, provided the:
                  • EFTA country does not prohibit imports of such waste; or
                  • the competent authority in the country of dispatch is satisfied that the waste will be managed in an environmentally sound manner.
                  (Article 34.)
                • Exports of hazardous waste for recovery, from an EU member state to a non-OECD country to which the OECD Decision does not apply. This also applies to certain other types of waste (Article 36).
                • Exports of non-hazardous waste for recovery, from an EU member state to a non-OECD country to which the OECD Decision does not apply. Certain wastes listed in Annexes to the Regulation can be exported to certain non-OECD countries. Amending Regulations can change the details of the Annexes (Articles 37-39).
                • Export of any waste from an EU member state to the Antarctic (Article 39).
                • Exports of waste for disposal, from an EU member state to its overseas countries or territories (Article 40). Some exports of hazardous waste for recovery from an EU member state to its overseas countries or territories are also prohibited. In this case, the provisions of Article 36 apply as relevant.
                • Imports of waste for disposal in an EU member state, from outside the EU (Articles 41). This is subject to exceptions, including where the country of dispatch:
                  • is a signatory to the Basel Convention; or
                  • has signed a bilateral or multilateral agreement with the EU or its member states to a member state under the Basel Convention (see Basel Convention: bilateral agreements and Basel Convention: multilateral agreements).
                • Imports of waste for recovery in an EU member state, from outside the EU (Article 43). This is subject to exceptions, including where the country of dispatch:
                  • is covered by the OECD Decision;
                  • is a signatory to the Basel Convention; or
                  • has signed a bilateral or multilateral agreement with the EU or its member states to a member state under the Basel Convention.
                In exceptional circumstances, imports of waste for recovery or disposal in an EU member state from outside the EU may be permitted, even if there is no bilateral or multilateral agreement in place. This applies in times of crisis, war or peacekeeping.

                When is a shipment destined for disposal and when is it destined for recovery?

                Recovery and disposal are mutually exclusive activities. If a shipment of waste is to undergo a series of operations in the country of destination, it is important to know whether these operations mean that the shipment is destined for disposal or recovery. This will determine which controls apply under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006.
                In SITA EcoService Nederland BV v Minister van Volkshuisvesting, Ruimtelijke Ordening en Milieubeheer (Case C-116/01) [2003] EUECJ C-116/01, the ECJ held that whether a shipment was for recovery or disposal depended on the nature of the first operation that the waste would undergo when it arrived at its destination, rather than any subsequent processing or disposal of residues from the initial operation.
                In 2012, the Court of Appeal gave its judgment in Ezeemo and others v R [2012] EWCA Crim 2064 (see Legal update, Court of Appeal considers transfrontier shipment of waste offences). The court said that the list of recovery operations set out in Annex IIB to the Waste Framework Directive should be construed "generously" or widely, so as to give effect to the intention of Article 36 of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006. In addition, Article 3 requires member states to encourage recovery of waste "by means of recycling, re-use or reclamation". Re-use of waste is therefore a possible form of recovery of waste.

                Tracking and Article 18 controls

                Consignments of green list waste intended for recovery, or waste that is destined for laboratory analysis to assess its suitability for recovery or disposal, do not need to be notified. However such waste must be accompanied by the tracking documentation required by Article 18 and set out in Annex VII to the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006.
                In 2012, the ECJ clarified the interaction between the:
                • Article 18(1) and Annex VII requirements to disclose information about waste producers in tracking documentation.
                • Article 18(4) duty of confidentiality and general EU law principles of business confidentiality.
                (Interseroh Scrap and Metal Trading GmbH v Sonderabfall-Management-Gesellschaft Rheinland-Pfalz mbH (SAM) (Case C-1/11) [2012] EUECJ C-1/11.)
                The ECJ considered that the principles of confidentiality did not restrict the scope of the duty to provide the information (see Legal update, Clarification of confidentiality and obligation to disclose waste producer in shipment documents (ECJ)).

                Duty to protect the environment

                Article 49 of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 requires all undertakings involved in the shipment of waste and its disposal or recovery to take the necessary steps to ensure that the waste is managed in a way that will not endanger human health and in an environmentally sound manner throughout the entire period of shipment and during its recovery or disposal.
                The requirement for environmentally sound management can usually be assumed to be met if the waste is recovered or disposed of according to environmental standards that are broadly equivalent to those under EU environmental law. Annex VIII sets out guidelines for what constitutes environmentally sound management.

                What amounts to a shipment?

                Under EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, a shipment is defined by reference to the countries, territories or other geographic area that the waste moves between (Article 2).
                The ECJ has held that a shipment actually starts from the point of departure of the waste in the state of dispatch and lasts until the completion of its treatment in the state of destination (EU-Wood case, see Objections to shipments above). This would appear to cover movements of waste using transportation before the transport (for example, on a lorry or a ship) that takes the waste out of the member state in question.
                For information on what the Court of Appeal in the UK decided about the meaning of export, see Courts consider the meaning of export below.

                Is shipment of discontinued or defective products a shipment of waste?

                In Tronex BV (C-624/17) EU:C:2019:564, the ECJ said that shipment to a third country of electrical and electronic appliances:
                • Was a shipment of waste where the good working condition of the appliances had not been checked or where they were not adequately protected from transport damage. The defective products could only be exempt from the Waste Shipments Regulation ((EC) 1013/2006) if the holder of the products could demonstrate not only that they could be reused, but that their reuse was certain.
                • Was not a shipment of waste where the appliances were discontinued goods that were still in their unopened original packaging (unless there were any indications that the appliances were waste).
                The court also clarified that a consumer returning defective goods under a product guarantee was not intending to discard the goods by disposing of them or carrying out a recovery operation. For more information, see Legal update, Clarification of whether a consignment of shipped goods is waste when some are defective and some are discontinued (ECJ)).

                Mixed waste

                Article 19 of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 prohibits mixing waste from the start of the shipment to its receipt in a disposal or recovery facility.
                However, certain waste that is already mixed may be shipped, subject to the following notification requirements:
                • Green list wastes that are mixed have to be notified in accordance with the prior written notification procedure in Article 4 (Article 3(1)(b)).
                • Mixtures where the composition of the mixture does not affect the environmentally sound recovery do not have to be notified, providing the mixtures are listed in Annex IIIA (Article 3(2)(b)).
                Most mixtures of green list wastes will be notifiable if separately catalogued in the European List of Wastes (see Practice note, Waste regime overview, key concepts and definitions).
                Regulation No 664/2011 amending Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 on shipments of waste to include certain mixtures of wastes in Annex IIIA thereto has amended Annex IIIA (see Legal update, Shipment of waste: European Commission Regulation published in Official Journal).

                Interaction with animal by-products (ABPs) regime

                In 2019, in ReFood GmbH & Co. KG v Landwirtschaftskammer Niedersachsen (C-634/17), the ECJ interpreted Article 1(3)(d) of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 as meaning that shipments of animal by-products (ABPs) falling within the EU ABP Regulation 2009 ((EC) No 1069/2009) were excluded from the scope of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, except where expressly provided otherwise. The court said that a contrary interpretation would cause uncertainty for operators and disregard the scheme of the EU ABP Regulation 2009, which was to lay down rules proportionate to the risks presented by the transport of different categories of ABPs, according to how hazardous they were. Except for the most hazardous ABPs, those rules were less strict than those contained in the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 (see Legal update, Interaction between transfrontier shipments of waste and animal by-products regimes clarified (ECJ)).
                For more information on the ABP regime, see Practice note, Animal by-products regulatory regime: summary.

                Proposal for certification scheme

                In 2013, the Commission launched a consultation on introducing a certification scheme for the transfrontier shipment of waste from EU countries to non-OECD countries, to ensure waste arising in the EU is treated in an environmentally-sound manner outside the EU as well as within. For more information, see Legal update, European Commission consults on certification scheme for transfrontier shipments of waste.

                EU Directive on port reception facilities for ship waste and cargo residues

                Directive (EU) 2019/883 of the European Parliament (EP) and of the Council of 17 April 2019 on port reception facilities for the delivery of waste from ships, amending Directive 2010/65/EU and repealing Directive 2000/59/EC (Ships Waste Directive 2019) came into force on 27 June 2019.
                The European Commission adopted its proposal for this Directive in January 2018 (see Legal update, Ship waste and cargo residues: European Commission adopts proposal repealing Port Facilities Directive 2000/59/EC).
                The Ships Waste Directive 2019 aims to protect the marine environment against the negative effects from discharges of waste from ships using EU ports, while ensuring the smooth operation of maritime traffic, by improving the availability and use of adequate port reception facilities and the delivery of waste to those facilities.
                Under the new rules, ships will have to pay an indirect fee, which will give them the right to deliver their waste to a port, and which will have to be paid regardless of whether or not they deliver any waste. This fee will also apply to fishing vessels and recreational craft, which means that it will also help prevent end-of-life fishing nets and passively fished waste going directly into the sea. The fee will be based on the principle of cost recovery. Landlocked member states which do not have ports or ships flying their flag will not be obliged to transpose the Directive or certain parts of it.
                Member states are required to bring into force the laws, regulations and administrative provisions necessary to comply by 28 June 2021. The Commission will evaluate this Directive and submit the results of the evaluation to the EP and the Council by 28 June 2026. The evaluation will also include a report detailing best waste prevention and management practices on board ships (see Legal update, Ship waste and cargo residues: Directive (EU) 2019/883 on port reception facilities for the delivery of waste from ships published in Official Journal).

                EU Ship Recycling Regulation 2013

                The EU Ship Recycling Regulation 2013 (Regulation (EU) 1257/2013 of the European Parliament and of the Council on ship of 20 November 2013 on ship recycling and amending Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 and Directive 2009/16/EC) amended the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 to implement the rules of the 2009 Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships (which is not yet in force). The Hong Kong Convention is overseen by the International Maritime Organization (IMO). The EU Ship Recycling Regulation 2013 aims to improve ship recycling conditions for EU-flagged ships worldwide by prompting the upgrade of ship recycling facilities to the standards included in the Regulation.
                Among other things, the EU Ship Recycling Regulation requires all large sea-going vessels sailing under an EU member state flag to use an approved ship recycling facility by 31 December 2018 (see Legal update, Ship recycling: Regulation (EU) 1257/2013 on ship recycling published in Official Journal).
                In accordance with Article 16(1), the Commission has adopted Implementing Acts to establish a European List of ship recycling facilities that are located in the EU and have been notified by the member states, and of ship recycling facilities located in a third country and whose inclusion in the list is based on an assessment in accordance with Article 15 of the Regulation (see Legal update, Ship recycling: five European Commission Implementing Decisions in relation to Regulation (EU) 1257/2013 published in Official Journal
                Article 5 also obliges all ships visiting European ports, irrespective of the flag they fly, to carry on board an Inventory of Hazardous Materials from the end of 2020 onwards. According to the Commission, this measure is expected to improve sourcing of quality ship parts globally and benefit European shipbuilding yards.

                UK transfrontier shipment of waste regulations

                Transfrontier Shipment of Waste Regulations 2007

                The EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 directly transposes the Basel Convention and the OECD Decision into UK law (see International legislation above). An EU Regulation has direct effect and so does not need to be transposed into the national law of members states. However, national legislation is necessary to specify the competent authorities and to set out offences and penalties for breach of the EU Regulation.
                The Transfrontier Shipment of Waste Regulations 2007 (SI 2007/1711) (TSWR 2007) specify the competent authorities for the UK for the purposes of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 and set out offences and penalties for breaching it.
                The TSWR 2007 came into force in July 2007 and revoked the Transfrontier Shipment of Waste Regulations 1994 (SI 1994/1137).
                For more information, see Legal update, New Regulations on the transfrontier shipment of waste.
                They have been amended a number of times, including by the:
                • Transfrontier Shipment of Waste (Amendment) Regulations 2008 (SI 2008/9) (TSWR 2008), which provides for additional offences (see Offences and penalties below).
                • Transfrontier Shipment of Waste (Amendment) Regulations 2014 (SI 2014/861) (TSWR 2014), which amends the competent authorities (see Legal update, Regulations to improve enforcement of transfrontier shipment of waste laid before Parliament and government publishes consultation response).

                Competent authorities in England and Wales

                From 1 May 2014, the TSWR 2014 amend the TSWR 2007 so that the competent authorities are as follows:
                • The Environment Agency (EA) is the competent authority where the shipment starts or ends in, or transits, England.
                • The EA replaces the Secretary of State as the competent authority for waste transiting the UK for disposal or recovery elsewhere.
                • Natural Resources Wales (NRW) is the competent authority where the shipment starts or ends in Wales.
                • Each UK competent authority replaces the Secretary of State as the competent authority for its own jurisdiction for waste moved, to or from, the marine area.
                (Regulation 6, TSWR 2007)

                Other enforcement powers

                The TSWR 2014 amend the TSWR 2007, from 1 May 2014, to provide the following powers to other bodies:
                • Authorise HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) to disclose relevant export data to the UK competent authorities, to help them develop better intelligence on illegal waste exports.
                • Authorise the Department for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy (BEIS) (formerly known as the Department of Energy and Climate Change (DECC)) to undertake inspections and gather evidence at offshore installations under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, on behalf of the UK competent authorities.
                • Authorise the Border Force to stop and detain suspect containers.

                Charging for notification procedure

                The TSWR 2007 also amend the Environment Act 1995 to allow the competent authority to establish charging schemes to cover their responsibilities under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 for notifiable and green list waste.
                Fees must be paid by the notifier when it submits a notification and the competent authority may refuse to proceed with a notification unless the required fee is submitted (regulation 46).

                Offences and penalties

                It is an offence to ship waste or undertake recovery or disposal of waste unless this is done in an environmentally sound manner and with the correct documentation (regulations 17 and 18, TSWR 2007).
                The TSWR 2008 amended the TSWR 2007 to add additional offences of:
                • Failure to comply with Regulation (EC) No 1418/2007 (Regulation (EC) No 1418/2007 concerning the export for recovery of certain waste listed in Annex III or IIIA to Regulation (EC) No 1013/2006 to certain countries to which the OECD Decision on the control of transboundary movements of wastes does not apply). Regulation (EC) No 1418/2007 sets out the specific requirements that apply to the export of waste listed in Annex III or IIIA to the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 (regulation 23A, TSWR 2007).
                • Failure to comply with Article 37(5) of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 (regulation 23B). Article 37(5) applies the procedure of prior written notification and consent to the export of certain types of waste for recovery in countries to which the OECD Decision does not apply.
                • Failure to comply with Article 37(4) of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, which requires such waste to be sent to facilities that are authorised in the country of destination (regulations 23A(4) and 23B(2)(b)).
                A person committing an offence under the TSWR 2007 is liable:
                • On conviction in a magistrates' court:
                  • for offences committed before 12 March 2015, by up to three months' imprisonment and/or a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum (£5,000); and
                  • for offences committed on or after 12 March 2015, by up to three months' imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine.
                • On conviction in the Crown Court, to an unlimited fine, to up to two years' imprisonment or to both.
                (Regulation 58.)
                An example of a significant fine for these offences is that, in September 2019, Wood Green Crown Court fined Biffa Waste Services Ltd £350,000 and ordered it to pay prosecution costs of £240,000 and a further £9,912 under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA) (see Legal update, £600,000 fine and costs for waste export offences).

                UK waste shipment plan

                The Secretary of State is required to prepare a waste management plan containing the policies on shipment of waste for disposal into or out of UK (regulation 11, TSWR 2007).
                The UK Plan for Shipments of Waste was republished in May 2012 with a minor amendment. It had originally been published in July 2007, when it replaced a 1994 plan on exports and imports of waste.
                The amendment in the republished version allows for the possible shipment of contaminated river sediments that are classified as non-hazardous waste. Legal references have also been updated.
                The UK Plan for Shipments of Waste is legally binding and sets out the government's policy on exports from, and imports to, the UK of waste for disposal and recovery. It provides technical guidance to the UK competent authorities when taking decisions on proposed waste shipments.
                Generally, waste shipments from and to the UK are prohibited, unless there is an exception that covers the situation in question. Examples of some of the exceptions include:
                • Emergency situations where the shipment of hazardous waste to the UK from any country is required because of a clear and immediate risk to human health or the environment.
                • Trial runs to the UK from any country to test a specific specialised treatment technology for the disposal of waste. This exception will not apply where the technology to be tested exists in the country of dispatch.

                Resources and Waste Strategy for England 2018

                The Resources and Waste Strategy (Our Waste, Our Resources, A Strategy for England), published by Defra in December 2018 included an action to improve the quality of plastics exported for recycling through the Basel and Stockholm Conventions. The government will launch a call for evidence in 2019.
                For more information on the Resources and Waste Strategy, see Practice note, Resources and Waste Strategy for England.

                Case law

                In R v Ideal Waste Paper Co Ltd and others [2011] EWCA Crim 3237, the defendants sought unsuccessfully to stay a criminal trial for illegal export of waste contrary to regulation 23 of the TSWR 2007 (see Legal update, Court of Appeal holds that abuse of process not established in waste export case). The defendants argued that there was an abuse of process because the lack of detailed technical guidance on acceptable levels of contamination of waste paper for export meant that the trial judge would be unable to direct the jury properly. Therefore, they argued, a fair trial would not be possible. The defendants appealed, but were unsuccessful.
                In Ezeemo and others v R [2012] EWCA Crim 2064, the Court of Appeal clarified that breach of regulation 23 of the TSWR 2007 is a strict liability offence (see Legal update, Court of Appeal considers transfrontier shipment of waste offences). The defendants were fined over £220,000 (see Legal update, Largest hazardous WEEE export prosecution by Environment Agency).

                Courts consider the meaning of export

                The Court of Appeal considered various aspects of the transfrontier shipment of waste legislation in KV and others v R [2011] EWCA Crim 2342. The court decided that:
                • The prohibitions in EU and UK legislation on the export of certain wastes from an EU member state for recovery in a non-OECD country should be interpreted as applying to all parties that are involved in transporting waste for export. Waste can be destined for recovery in a non-OECD country long before it actually leaves the EU. The action of waste leaving the EU (that is, exporting waste) is a process that commences when the waste is destined for a non-OECD country, and continues until the waste reaches its ultimate destination in that country.
                • Regulation 23 of the TSWR 2007 is not wider in scope than Article 23 of the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006, nor is it disproportionate.
                For more information, see Legal update, Court of Appeal considers application of transfrontier shipment of waste legislation.

                Transfrontier shipment of radioactive waste: summary

                The transfrontier shipment of radioactive waste is regulated by:
                • Directive 2006/117/Euratom on the supervision and control of shipments of radioactive waste and spent fuel.
                • Regulation (Euratom) No 1493/93 on shipments of radioactive substances between member states.
                • Directive 2011/70/Euratom establishing a Community framework for the responsible and safe management of spent fuel and radioactive waste. Member states had until 23 August 2013 to implement Directive 2011/70/Euratom. Its provisions include that where radioactive waste or spent fuel is shipped for processing or reprocessing, either within the EU or outside, the ultimate responsibility for the disposal of those materials, including any waste as a by-product, will remain with the country from which the radioactive material was shipped (see Legal update, EU radioactive waste Directive published in the Official Journal: Shipping of radioactive waste).
                For:
                • Background information, see Report from the Commission to the European Parliament, the Council and the European Economic and Social Committee on the implementation by the Member States of Council Directive 2006/117 EURATOM on the supervision and control of shipments of radioactive waste and spent fuel.
                • More information on nuclear safety, see Practice note, International and EU nuclear safety and nuclear liability regimes.

                Guidance and further reading

                The EA has published an unofficial consolidated list of green and amber waste under the EU Waste Shipments Regulation 2006 (see EA: Unofficial consolidated version of Annexes III, IIIA, IIIB IV & IVA of Regulation (EC) No. 1013/2006 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 14 June 2006 on shipments of wastes).
                It has also published a waste export tool, to help operators determine the regulatory controls that apply to the exports of wastes for recovery to countries outside England and Wales.
                For more information on the transfrontier shipment of waste, see:
                • EA: International waste shipments.
                • EA: Waste import and export.
                • European Commission: Waste shipments.
